### What's the difference between Lithium-ion and lithium polymer 

**Lithium-ion Batteries:**
- Widely used in gadgets for decades due to their long life, high energy density, and cost-effectiveness.
- Rechargeable using the reversible reduction of lithium ions with a liquid electrolyte.
- Merits:
    - Cost-effective due to mature technology and mass production.
    - Higher energy density: stores 3-4 times more charge than similar-sized batteries.
    - Long lifespan: lasts 2–3 years or 300–500 charge cycles.
    - Low maintenance and maintains constant voltage.

- Demerits:
    - Efficiency decreases over time; performance degrades with age.
    - Fragile—prone to damage due to the liquid electrolyte (can puncture easily).
    - Unsafe at high temperatures; risk of fire if overheated.

**Lithium Polymer Batteries:**
- Newer technology (since the 1970s), uses a solid polymer electrolyte.
- Called Li-Po, versatile, and can be made in various shapes and sizes.
- Merits:
    - Safer and lightweight due to flexible packaging and absence of liquid electrolyte.
    - Versatile in shape; customizable for manufacturers.
    - Low self-discharge rate—retains charge when not in use.
    - Thin design with reasonable capacity (10–15% more capacity than similar-size batteries).

- Demerits:
    - More expensive than Li-ion batteries.
    - Lower energy density and fewer charge cycles than Li-ion.
    - Shorter lifespan—decays faster than Li-ion.

**Key Point:**  
Li-ion is cheaper, has higher energy density, and lasts longer, but is more prone to damage and overheating. Li-Po is safer, lighter, and more customizable, but more expensive and has a shorter life and lower energy density.
